Alberta Judiciary

Appointed judges across Alberta's court hierarchy — including appointment dates, known leanings, and notable rulings.

Court of Justice

Founded: 1907 (renamed from Provincial Court in 2024) Appointing Authority: Lieutenant Governor in Council (Alberta Cabinet) Judges: ~136 province-wide

Handles the vast majority of cases in Alberta — summary criminal offences, family law, civil claims under $100,000, youth justice, and traffic matters. Judges are recommended by a provincial judicial appointments committee and appointed by the Alberta Cabinet. No jury trials at this level. The court sits in communities across the province, making it the most accessible point of entry into the justice system for most Albertans.

Court of King's Bench

Founded: 1907 Appointing Authority: Federal Governor in Council on recommendation of the Commissioner for Federal Judicial Affairs Judges: ~75

Alberta's superior trial court. Presides over serious criminal matters (including jury trials), major civil litigation, divorce proceedings, and appeals from the Court of Justice. Because judges are federally appointed, their selection reflects the priorities of the federal government in power at the time of appointment — a distinction with significant political implications in a province with persistent federal-provincial tensions. Decisions are binding on the Court of Justice.

Court of Appeal

Founded: 1921 Appointing Authority: Federal Governor in Council on recommendation of the Commissioner for Federal Judicial Affairs Judges: ~15

The highest court in Alberta. Hears appeals from the Court of King's Bench and select administrative tribunals. Cases are typically decided by three-judge panels; significant matters may be heard by five. All federally appointed. Decisions are binding on every lower court in Alberta and carry persuasive weight in other provinces. Appeals beyond this court go to the Supreme Court of Canada by leave only.

Supreme Court of Canada

Founded: 1875 Appointing Authority: Prime Minister (no formal vetting process — sole discretion of the Prime Minister) Justices: 9 nationally

Canada's final court of appeal. Decisions are binding on every court in the country. Justices are appointed directly by the Prime Minister with no parliamentary confirmation process — one of the most unchecked appointments in Canadian government. This page highlights justices with Alberta connections and landmark cases that originated from Alberta. The court's rulings on resource rights, Indigenous law, and the Charter have had outsized effects on Alberta specifically.
